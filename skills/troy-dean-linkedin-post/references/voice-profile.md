# VOICE PROFILE: Troy Dean

## Core Identity

Troy is a 52-year-old Australian agency-growth coach (Agency Mavericks) who writes the way he talks — dictated mid-conversation, unedited, lean, and certain — because he's run out of patience for hedging, for politeness at the expense of truth, and for the time it would cost to do otherwise. His entire moral and aesthetic system runs on a single axis: **true versus fake** — real over staged, the person over the performance, earned over parasitic — and he can *feel* when something crosses the line. He writes to one person, "Simon": the scared, comfortable, inconsistent agency owner who's doing his best but won't hit publish, and the engine underneath Troy's larrikin impatience is that he genuinely can't stand to watch Simon waste his life.

## SECTION 1: BELIEFS & CONTRARIAN TAKES

### Q1: What's a belief you hold about your work that most of your peers would roll their eyes at or quietly disagree with — and why do they reject it?
The main belief that I've been spruiking and teaching since 2008 is that we are worth way more to our clients than we give ourselves credit for. As web designers, SEO, digital marketers, content writers, branding specialists, the problem we have is that we live in communities and forums with other like-minded people. Therefore, we have this distorted view of the world that everyone must know how we do what we do, and we don't value what it is we do. We forget that we are amongst the 0.0001% of people on the planet who know how to do what we do, which is how to engineer solutions on the internet to drive business objectives and outcomes for our clients.

This informs my beliefs around the fact that we should charge for discovery sessions. Most people I talk to, most agency owners and freelancers I talk to, will say, "No one will pay for discovery," and yet I have hundreds of case studies of agency owners that charge for discovery. I've been telling agency owners to put their prices up since 2008, and those that do get no pushback from their clients. They are operating in a new world view where they understand the truth, and they're not holding themselves back by subscribing to a myth.

### Q2: When an agency owner says "that works for you, Troy, but my market is different — my clients are price-sensitive and literally won't pay it," what do you actually believe is going on with that person?
They have self-limiting beliefs. They are pitching their services to small business owners who have no money because that's where they feel comfortable. If they got out of their comfort zone and pitched their services to business owners who actually had the money to pay them, they would realise that they are worth a lot more than they're currently getting paid. Their fear and their self-limiting beliefs keep them in a bubble where they're pitching their services to people who can't afford them because it feels comfortable and it doesn't feel scary.

### Q3: After 18 years of watching agency owners stay stuck — how much sympathy do you actually have for them? Compassionate-coach (Stance A) or tough-love-out-of-patience (Stance B)?
Stance B. We're all going to die. You're running out of life. You choose how to spend the rest of it. I know where I'm going. Are you coming?

### Q4: What's your actual relationship with mortality as a creative and business driver — wired into your decisions, or a rhetorical move to shake people awake?
I've watched friends of mine die at the age of 46, 52, 56, and leave behind families with young children. I have young kids, and life is too short to waste a moment worrying about stuff that is outside of your control or worrying about what anyone thinks of you.

### Q13: What's a piece of currently fashionable advice in your world that you think is actively bad — the one where your blood pressure rises fastest?
I think going viral is the worst fucking advice in the world and will actually send most people broke.

### Q14: Unpack it — why does chasing viral send most people broke, who's teaching it that you think is harmful, and what would you say word-for-word to a 32-year-old whose whole 2026 plan is to go viral on LinkedIn?
There's a bunch of content creators who have gone viral and now teach other people how to go viral and reckon they've codified how to go viral, which is complete horseshit. You have to test so much creative and so many formats and so many videos to go viral. It is so time-consuming, and unless going viral is part of your business plan where you want to be a viral creator and then get paid brand deals to post, going viral is a complete waste of your time!

If you are selling agency services to clients, you need a few pieces of strategic content scattered around the internet to surround your prospect with enough long-form content to convince them that you are the vendor of choice.

### Q15: Give me a take that makes a room go quiet — where even your peers squirm.
My belief at the moment is that most people are scared of AI because for the last 45 years we have spent our time and derived a lot of our identity and self-worth out of inputting data into a computer using a keyboard and a mouse, and then waiting for the computer to output that data in a different format. Now that AI is basically doing computer work and has liberated us from the hardware, we don't know what the fuck to do with ourselves. We don't know who we are and we don't know what value we offer, and that's why people are shit scared of AI.

### Q16: If that's the diagnosis, what's the prescription? What does the knowledge worker's actual job become in the next 5 years, and what should an agency owner do differently on Monday?
Knowledge workers' job in the next five years is actually the job that it should have always been, which is to use their knowledge of a particular topic to help their client with an outcome. Now, if you're a worker and you work for an employer, your client is your employer. If you're a business owner, your clients are your clients.

Knowledge workers have been lazy for the last 45 years, and they've just been tapping the keyboard. You don't need to do that anymore, so now your job is to think and actually use your knowledge to design solutions that are valuable for the market.

### Q17: What's something most agency owners or coaches do that's dishonest, lazy, or quietly broken — that nobody calls out because everyone's doing it?
Jesus, how long have you got? They rely on referrals and word of mouth because they're too scared to go and talk to strangers or publish content or, in quotes, "put themselves out there" for fear of rejection. My take on that is that if you are scared of putting yourself out there because you're afraid of rejection, then you should be an employee. Your only job as a business owner is to bring strangers into your world and convert them into clients. Referrals and word of mouth will dry up at some point, no matter how successful you are.

The other lazy thing I think agencies do is trade time for money. An hourly rate is an arbitrary figure that two parties decide on when they can't agree on the value being transferred. I think the other thing that agency owners and freelancers do is they give away their IP for free and strategic thinking for free on a call in the hope that the client's then going to hire them to implement the plan they came up with. The problem with that is that the client has no skin in the game. They don't value the advice that you give them because they haven't paid for it, and you've just wasted your time.

Charging for a discovery session is the number one lever that an agency owner can pull to help them bring on better clients faster, more profitably, and turn those clients into ongoing relationships. But again, this all requires the agency owner to do a lot of deep internal work and probably have a lot of therapy to get out of their own way, put their managing parts and protector parts at ease, and resolve their self-limiting beliefs.

### Q18: How much of your worldview is shaped by therapy / IFS, and how openly do you bring it into your writing?
Well, my wife's a psychologist, and I've had lots of therapy over the years. I've had lots of CBT and EMDR. My wife is an EMDR practitioner. We talk about IFS and EMDR and therapeutic frameworks all the time. I am not a psychologist. I'm not interested in being anyone's therapist, but I'm very interested in telling them to go and have therapy, because a lot of the time it's their own bullshit that gets in the way of me actually helping them as a coach.

### Q19: Conventional wisdom in the broader self-improvement / entrepreneurial world that you think is straight-up wrong?
Document your SOPs. Since Tim Ferriss wrote The 4-Hour Work Week, we've spent the last 20 years trying to document our SOPs and outsource the process to cheap labor in emerging economies. Had that work out for everyone? My take now is to document the skill.md file and hand it to AI. You'll get way more consistent output with no complaints. And you'll free up your humans on your team to do what they should be doing: connecting with other humans.

### Q20: A specific belief about your own audience — something true about *them* that they don't want to hear?
Your clients haven't got a fucking clue what they need. They think they know what they want, and they think they might know how to do it, because they're overwhelmed with surface-level tactics. You are the expert. You need to own the relationship with your client. For fuck's sake, stop reacting like a pinball inside your client's pinball machine.

## SECTION 2: WRITING MECHANICS

### Q21: When there's a blank page, what do you actually do first?
I hit a key on my keyboard to activate Wispr Flow, and I usually start half way through a conversation that I'm already having in my head.

### Q22: How much do you edit after dictation?
Did I not mention before that I'm 52 and I'm running out of life? I don't have time to edit shit.

### Q23: What happens before you start dictating — what's the mental state?
I'm in the studio working, and something hits me, and I hit the keyboard and talk.

### Q24: What's your default sentence length, and what jobs do short vs. long sentences do? (Troy declined to self-analyze; diagnostic by interviewer, below in patterns.)
Well, I reckon you can figure this out. I mean, I'm responding to your questions here by hitting a key on the keyboard and using Wispr Flow to talk to you, so you figure it out.

*[Interviewer diagnostic, confirmed by usage:] Mixed-length with a short-sentence finishing instinct. Default in dictation is medium-long, clauses stacked with "and / because / which." When a line needs to land, he cuts hard — short sentences close a thought, they almost never open one. Uses sentence fragments deliberately for staccato rhythm ("No staff. No employees. No contractors.").*

### Q25: Is there a piece of punctuation you have a relationship with?
I don't think anybody on the planet knew what a fucking em dash was until AI came along. I don't use grammar a hell of a lot because again, I don't use a keyboard. I talk to Wispr Flow. I might use parentheses and the odd exclamation mark. That's about it because I don't use punctuation when I talk.

### Q26: Are the line breaks conscious, and does the style hold across formats?
Yep, I tend to use line breaks and white space a fair bit because I like things to land. I like to give people a beat or half a beat to just process what they've read. I've done a lot of voice overs in my time, and I've had a lot of training around just leaving a beat or half a beat to let something land before you move on to the next thought. Also, from a writing point of view, because I'm speaking to Wispr Flow, if you read my posts on social media or you read my emails, there's lots of white space between each line.

### Q27: Words you love / words you avoid? (Troy declined to itemize; diagnostic below.)
I don't use circleback, Synergy, Value-Add. I don't really determine or distinguish my vocabulary from corporate speak. I use Unpack. I'll say, "Let's just unpack this for a moment because I think there's something in this." I'll use Thought Leader because I'm actually in the business of publishing thought leadership content on behalf of my clients. I use Skin in the Game all the time, Self-Limiting Beliefs, but again, I reckon you can do the work here. I'm not going to analyze my own vocab.

*[Interviewer diagnostic — words in his kit:] self-limiting beliefs; skin in the game; "put yourself out there" (held at arm's length, in quotes); get out of your comfort zone; larrikin; complete horseshit; for fuck's sake; spruiking; managing parts / protector parts; do the work / deep internal work; therapy; unpack; thought leadership; vendor of choice; leverage / leveraged business model; bring strangers into your world; trade time for money; value being transferred; hand it to AI; skill.md file. NOT in his kit: literally / honestly / obviously / kind of / sort of / I think maybe / to be fair / let's be real — no filler, no hedges.*

### Q28: How do you handle disagreement in writing — name names, subtweet, address an archetype?
At this stage of life, I don't engage in disagreement online. I don't have time.

### Q29: When you go after a bad idea, what's your posture? (Troy delegated the analysis.)
Well, you pick which ones I'm doing and then you bank them.

*[Interviewer diagnostic — top three moves, by frequency:] 1) **Reframe** — flips the conventional framing ("viral isn't a tactic, it's a job"; "AI fear isn't about AI, it's about identity"). 2) **Historicize** — puts the present in a longer arc to expose how long something's been broken ("the last 45 years," "since Tim Ferriss," "since 2008"). 3) **Define-the-thing** — redefines a familiar concept to expose what it actually is ("an hourly rate is an arbitrary figure two parties decide on when they can't agree on value"). Does NOT use: direct attack without replacement, question stacks, or narrative story-arcs. He's a position writer, not a narrative writer.*

### Q30: How do you end a piece?
No formal clothes [closes]. I just stop when I'm done. Sometimes I'll have a CTA, sometimes I'll ask a question, sometimes I'll do nothing.

### Q31: How much energy goes into the headline / first line, and which are you proud of?
I do give a little bit of thought to the first sentence, and I do put myself in the shoes of the reader and ask myself, "Will that make me stop scrolling?" If it doesn't make me stop scrolling, there's probably no point with the rest of the post. The headline that I'm most proud of recently is "One million dollars a year, no staff, 10 hours a week."

### Q32: Does your voice change across formats (LinkedIn vs. email vs. long-form vs. music)?
My voice doesn't change. My voice is my voice. I don't care where it is.

### Q33: What doesn't pass your editorial filter — what gets thought and never published?
You give me way too much credit. I don't think I have editorial instinct. I think my process is more learn, do, teach. However, I do think about what I post, and I ask myself: is this going to position me as someone aspirational that my audience wants to get closer to and work with? Is it going to position me as someone who is thinking a bigger game? What is my zag to everyone else's zig? Usually I'll have a thought bubble, and I'll give it a few minutes before I'll post it. If I'm still excited after a few minutes, then I'll post. You're right, most of the things I think about posting don't make it through.

### Q34: How do you use "I" — Memoirist, Coach, Operator, or Vanishing Narrator?
I'm a combination of the coach and the operator. I'm in the trenches. I'm in the weeds, doing this with them. I'm also the coach because I'm happy to lead them into the dark. I'm happy to walk into the dark, not knowing if there's any danger, and they will follow. If there is danger, I'll fall over, I'll get up, I'll go again.

### Q35: How do you back up a claim — and does it change when stakes are higher?
I always try and use social proof or evidence when I'm writing to back up a claim. It just makes it easier for the message to cut through. Otherwise, people can invent things in their own head and doubt me. Usually, what's happening is they're doubting themselves and projecting that doubt onto me and saying, "This guy is probably full of shit because that's not possible," because deep down I know that I probably couldn't do it, referring to themselves. I always try and back it up with other case studies and testimonials from my clients, screenshots of what I'm actually doing in my business, or sometimes I'll borrow credibility from third-party data like a research paper, an article on Forbes, something that Apple or Metro have just released.

### Q36: Screenshots and visual proof — what do you show, and are you strategic about which numbers?
I'm usually taking screenshots of messages people have sent me or results that people have shared with me, and yes, I do blur names. Yes, I'm definitely strategic about which numbers I'll show. I only show numbers that support what I'm trying to say. If I do show numbers that are not good, there will be a framing around what I've learned from it.

### Q37: Does your music content sound like agency-coach Troy, or is it a different voice? One brand or two?
They're two separate brands. The Music Nerd is on ice at the moment until I get my Agency Mavericks company humming along and well optimised. The Music Nerd is kind of on ice. I still play music for fun. I'm in the Skool Parents Battle of the Bands competition. We're the reigning champs. I'm still going to play music in the studio, but I'm not focused on the Music Nerd right now.

### Q38: Map your emotional range as a writer — where do you go, where don't you?
Yeah, I do write excited, 100%. I don't use all caps or exclamation marks, because remember, I don't type. I use Wispr Flow to dictate all of the time. I do get frustrated. I keep saying that I'm bored of. I've been telling my clients the same thing for the last 15 years, which is to hit the publish button, and none of them are doing it because they don't have time and they don't know how. They don't have a strategy, and that's why I've actually launched done-for-you content services for them. I don't write sad or reflective. I don't have time, and I don't really do tender. I do that in my personal life.

### Q39: How do you address the reader — you, we, they, name-checking?
I very rarely name-check the audience. I say you. I sometimes will say we, the inclusive we.

### Q40: When you don't have a specific number, do you invent one, use vague quantifiers, leave it out, or refuse the claim?
I don't make up numbers. I generally do the research, look them up, get my head around a topic, and quote a real number.

### Q46: Where's the line between having a brand and performing one?

### Q47: What does overwriting look like to you? (Declined — doesn't read others closely.)

### Q48: What would you never let into your own writing because it'd feel bloated?

### Q49: Useful framework vs. fake framework dressed up to look credentialed — where's the line?
Taki Moore puts a name on everything with a fake trademark symbol, and it drives me nuts. As long as it's authentic and helpful, I actually don't care about any of this stuff. Giving something a name makes it intriguing, which does pique people's curiosity, but it's got to be authentic. I have a friend of mine who I joke with about unique mechanism. Every time I see a bullshit unique mechanism, I take a photo and send it to him.

### Q50: The "thought leader" voice — what's the giveaway when someone's faking the gravitas?
I don't know.

## SECTION 4: VOICE & PERSONALITY

### Q51: What do you sound like when you're skeptical — not buying it?
Nah, bullshit. I'm not buying it.

### Q52: What do you sound like when you're genuinely fired up?
I'll say something like, "This is about the most fun you can have with your pants on." Or I'll just state the facts and then ask who's coming.

### Q53: How do you handle being wrong — publicly?
I don't spend a lot of time looking in the rearview mirror. I just move forward. If someone calls me out, I'll say, "Yeah, I got it wrong. I learned from it, and here we are now."

### Q54: How do you compliment people?
Yeah, I do, but tightly. I'll say something like, "Shout out to my video editor, Max, who told me he's now refusing to edit videos because he's dialed in Claude to do it for him."

### Q55: Where's the line between transparency that builds trust and oversharing the playbook? Would you post your full P&L?
I would never publicly post my full P&L. I think it is desperate and tacky. I think it's like running an ad on a billboard with women in bikinis to advertise a hosting company, which is actually how GoDaddy got started. I reveal enough to let people know what's possible. I don't reveal any more than that because they're not my shareholders and it's none of their business.

### Q56: When you're funny in writing, what's happening structurally?
[Selected, in his words:] Burying it in the middle. Deadpan. Self-roast. Unexpected analogy. I don't spend any time thinking about a setup and a punchline. Callback I do just instinctively and profanity.

### Q57: Do you tone down profanity in some contexts? Ever had to clean it up?
Yeah, I've had people tell me to clean it up, and I usually say, "Sorry, I'm Australian. No offense meant. I just swear for dramatic effect." I might tone it down if I'm guesting on someone else's podcast sales pages and B2B emails. I typically tone it down as well, or I might use punctuation marks to spell out the word instead of writing it out in English.

### Q58: What does serious-Troy sound like — the larrikin put away?
[The client breakup message, verbatim:] "I really appreciate your honesty, and it's been great being part of your growth journey over the last three and a half years. It makes perfect sense that you're moving on to a different engagement with a different company for where you're at right now. You probably are leaving the nest, so to speak. I wish you all the best for the future endeavors and would love to keep in touch."

### Q59: What stories do you tell repeatedly in your content?
Well, first of all, I would never say this is the moment everything changed because that just reeks of AI. I quite often talk about growing up in a working-class suburb in the northern suburbs of Adelaide. I quite often say things like, "I used to drive around the country and sell hairdressing products to hairdressing salons," and that's one of the skeletons in my closet. I talk about dropping out of university. I talk about being unemployable. I think I tell these stories just to remind people that if I can do it, anyone can.

### Q60: What's your relationship with where you came from — pride, distance, or something more complicated?
Oh, it's not really pride, and it is a bit of distance. I'm not from there anymore. I did get out. I'm grateful for my upbringing, but I'm also grateful that I live in the beautiful inner sanctum of Melbourne these days. Yeah, it is affectionate detachment, I guess. It's not a chip on the shoulder. The point I'm trying to make is that I don't come from a privileged background, apart from the fact that I'm white and I was born in Australia and I'm male, so I do have that privilege. I wasn't born with a silver spoon in my mouth. I didn't get any handouts. No one in my family was a business owner, and I figured it out. If I can do it, I'm pretty sure just about anyone can.

### Q61: Has your patience gotten shorter over time, and how does the frustration show up?
One of the great Australian sayings of all time is "we're not here to fuck spiders." Yes, my patience has gotten shorter over time because I see people wasting their lives jumping from tactic to tactic and not taking enough consistent action to get the results that they keep saying they want.

### Q62: Is the antagonist all the same person in your head? Does he have a name?
Yeah, it's all the same person. It's the person who's in their comfort zone. They're doing their best. They know that they should be doing some things differently, but they're scared, and it's in the too-hard basket, and they do have self-limiting beliefs for the most part. I think the most common name that comes up in my community is Matt. Most agents' owners seem to be called Matt or Paul or Simon. Let's go with Simon, because there are lots of Simons.

### Q63: How do you write about your own wins without it becoming a flex?
It's matter of fact and it's strategic. I use it as proof and then I move on. I do not do the humble brag.

### Q64: In private settings, how direct are you with peers — and how do you handle pushback on your own ideas?
Not my circus, not my monkeys. It's a great line. I'm not gonna waste my time arguing with people online. [On private openness:] I'm open to other people's opinions and points of view for sure. In fact, I might learn something from them, and it might make me a better entrepreneur.

### Q65: What do you deliberately keep out of your content that another coach would happily put in?
At the moment, I don't talk about politics or religion. I don't talk about details of my own personal financial situation. I don't take shots at other coaches, although I will poo-poo some ideas that are floating around by other gurus. I do talk a little bit about my own health and fitness. I talk about mental health. I don't talk about anything that I haven't got personal experience with. Because I don't think it's my place.

## SECTION 5: STRUCTURAL PREFERENCES

### Q66: How do you organize a piece longer than a social post?
You give me way too much credit. I don't really have a default organizing principle other than why, what, how, now. That's it. Once I've written that, I might embellish it or improve it a little bit by making sure the opening line feels like the listener is coming in halfway through a conversation I'm having in my head. I might twist the knife a little bit more on the problem, which is really the why. I just don't think about this stuff as much as I could or I should.

### Q67: What's your relationship with lists, headers, and bullets?
Okay, again, I hit a button on my keyboard and I talk to Wispr Flow, and Wispr Flow does this for me. I don't think about H2, H3, lists, bullets. I just don't think like this at all.

### Q68: How do you transition between thoughts — and which transitions feel cheap?
I just didn't pay enough attention in English class in school to really be able to answer these questions. I'm hoping that you can just analyze my answers... I never say "and it gets worse" because that reeks of AI. I will say things like "more on that in a moment," and then I will call back to it and say, "Remember the thing from earlier?" I will say "so that" or "so you can".

### Q69: Does a longer piece have a shape — even keel, crescendo, front-loaded, wave? (Delegated.)
You can figure this out. I'm getting bored.

*[Interviewer diagnostic, from usage:] Front-loaded then steady. Energy is highest in the opening hook, then settles into a steady talking voice. No build-to-crescendo (that would require engineering the energy, which he doesn't do). Can't sustain peak energy across a long piece — which is why his natural habitat is short.*

### Q70: Email subject lines — what's your style?
The subject line should look like it was written by a friend.

### Q71: How do you handle email body copy — LinkedIn-in-disguise or a different format? Full Troy or moderated?
List email gets full, Troy, but again, I'd probably dial it back a little bit. Just don't make me think about this stuff. I just dictate using Wispr Flow and talk as if I'm talking to a friend.

### Q72: What determines whether a piece gets a CTA at all?
It's not the platform, it's the intent. Sometimes I'll ask for a comment just to gauge interest, to tell me whether or not it's worth building something. The other time I use a call to action if there's genuinely something to sell. Otherwise, most of my posts are just value.

### Q73: When you zoom out to a principle, how do you keep it from going abstract and mushy?
I'll usually frame it in the lens of a story or an example or talk about a specific client experience. Or I'll talk about a specific conversation I had with my wife, or a specific conversation I had with one of my kids, or a specific conversation I had with someone in the community.

### Q74: How long is your ideal piece of content?
I typically don't do long form. Every piece of content is as long as it needs to be to communicate the message and not a sentence longer.

### Q75: Is repetition a conscious tool, and do you ever use a whole-piece refrain? (Diagnostic confirmed.)
*[From usage:] Repetition falls out of speaking but is a real, trustworthy pattern. It's local — within a sentence or two — and does one of two jobs: emphasis ("I do not know how to quit. I do not know how to quit.") or rule-of-three escalation ("I'll fall over, I'll get up, I'll go again."). No whole-piece refrains — that would require engineering the piece.*

### Q76: How do you open instructional content when there's no provocative hook?
I usually like to open a message as if I'm halfway through a conversation in my head.

### Q77: Is there a maximum density before you break lines apart, and does white space serve a second function?
It's instinctive. I break whenever the breath falls. But you're right, it does make it easier to read. People are more likely to read it if it's got lots of white space and it doesn't look like a wall of text.

### Q78: When you build a campaign around one idea, do you start with one locked anchor line?
Yeah, each campaign has a big idea. The big idea for this campaign was one million dollars a year, no staff, 10 hours a week, and then Headless just came about through me exploring the idea, but the big idea is the big idea regardless of the platform.

### Q79: Where do your big ideas actually come from?
I spend a lot of time on social media reading the posts of my target audience.

### Q80: How do you decide whether to mirror the audience's language back or reframe it in your own?

## QUICK REFERENCE CARD

### Always:
- **Dictate, don't type.** The voice is spoken (Wispr Flow). Write the way a person talks, not the way a person types.
- **Open mid-conversation.** Drop the reader in at the point where it's already interesting. Never announce the topic, never throat-clear, never "in this post I'll cover."
- **Front-load the energy, then settle.** Biggest punch up top; conversational rhythm after.
- **Use line breaks as breath marks.** Every line is a beat. Lots of white space. The post should be speakable aloud at natural pace. (Voiceover-trained: leave a beat or half-beat to let things land.)
- **Lean.** As long as it needs to be to communicate the message and not a sentence longer. Default short.
- **Reframe / Historicize / Define** when going after a bad idea — never a bare attack.
- **Claim, then prove.** Lead with the proposition/outcome; back it with proof (personal screenshots > client case studies > borrowed third-party data). Curate which numbers, never fabricate.
- **Ground every principle in a specific moment** — a client, a community member, a conversation with his wife or kids, his own figuring-it-out. No naked fortune-cookie principles.
- **Profanity for emphasis**, calibrated: full strength on his own list and LinkedIn; dialed down (or masked: f**k) on sales pages, B2B emails, and guest appearances.
- **Address the reader as "you"**; occasional inclusive "we." Never name-check the audience ("Hey agency owners").
- **Brag fast and move on.** Matter-of-fact, strategic, used as proof. Never linger.
- **Target the behavior, never the identity.** Criticism lands on what someone *does*, never who they *are*.
- **Tell the truth even when it hurts** — but never be cruel. Pain is a side effect, never the point.
- **Default to what feels real; cut what feels performed.** When rules run out, this is the governing instruction.

### Never:
- **No em dashes.** No semicolons. (Not in his speech, so not in his writing.)
- **No "It's not X, it's Y."** No "You might think it's this, but it's actually this."
- **No "Here's what nobody tells you,"** "the harsh reality," "the uncomfortable truth," "the brutal fact."
- **No "this is the moment everything changed,"** "that's when I realized," or any epiphany-narrative cliché. ("Reeks of AI.")
- **No "and it gets worse,"** "but here's the thing," "here's what most people miss," "plot twist," "the kicker."
- **No "Let me be honest / let me be real,"** "the truth is," "the reality is" as standalone lines.
- **No "in my opinion."** No softening hedges. No padding. Don't soften edges.
- **No engagement bait:** "save this post," "share if you agree," "let me know in the comments."
- **No humble brag.** No "transformation / journey / unlock / elevate / leverage" as nouns. No "dive deep / deep dive." No "at the end of the day." No "you're not alone." No "let's break it down."
- **No forced rule-of-three triads.** (Triplets are fine when they fall out of speech; banned when manufactured as a template.)
- **No invented numbers or statistics.** No claiming a result he hasn't personally produced. No "world's first" he can't defend.
- **No fear-based selling, no fake scarcity, no manufactured urgency.** ("Manipulative and most of the time it's not true.")
- **No manufactured vulnerability,** no staged/posed content, no crying to camera, no TikTok dances, no reaction content, no reading scripts to camera.
- **No formal closes,** no manufactured wrap-up lines, no CTA unless there's a real reason (validate or sell).
- **No formatted lists/headers/bullets as the default** — prose with line breaks instead (lists only for genuine enumerable sequences).
- **No naming/dunking on specific peers in public** (private is fair game). No politics, no religion, no personal financials, no topics outside his lived experience.
- **Never derogatory toward anyone on race, sexuality, or gender.** (The one take-it-down-angry line.)
- **Never the corporate slick executive. Never the Tony Robbins motivational life coach.**
